Case Study  ·  The Humor Project

Computational Humor Research

AI, Language, and Human Judgment

TypeAI Product Engineer · The Humor Project
RoleEnd-to-end: LLM selection, prompt engineering, evaluation design, product iteration
UsersCollege students at Columbia, Barnard, and Stanford
ProblemGenerative AI optimizes for fluency, not emotional response — humor requires audience-aware evaluation
OpportunityTreating humor as a product problem (not just a language problem) enables measurable improvement
Key MetricsUser engagement rate · Content share rate · A/B prompt strategy win rate · Audience-specific resonance
01   Problem

Can AI generate humor that actually lands?

Most generative AI optimizes for fluency — not emotional response. A joke that works for one audience falls flat for another. This project explored whether AI could produce humor that resonates with real college students, built into a consumer app with a fast, unforgiving feedback loop.

"Humor is a product problem, not just a language problem — audience-aware prompting and iterative evaluation can measurably improve engagement."

02   Approach

End-to-end across product and engineering.

  • Selected LLMs for tone control and iteration speed; designed prompts encoding campus-specific context
  • Defined "good" using live user feedback rather than static labels — then ran A/B prompt experiments against real engagement data
  • Iterated continuously based on in-app testing and qualitative signals
03   Key Tradeoffs

Deliberate tradeoffs shaped the product.

  • Live feedback over labeled datasets. Real-time student reactions gave noisier but more honest signal than stale humor benchmarks.
  • Audience-specific prompts over generic generation. Targeted prompts with campus context consistently outperformed one-size-fits-all approaches.
  • Fast iteration over model optimization. Shipping prompt variants quickly and learning from A/B results beat deep single-model tuning.
  • Control over raw creativity. Structured output and tone control mattered more than maximizing novelty.
04   Outcome

What the project produced.

Audience-specific prompt strategies consistently outperformed generic approaches across engagement and share metrics. The feedback loop was the product — iterative human-in-the-loop evaluation beat one-shot optimization.

The same approach — audience-aware evaluation, rapid iteration, and clear success criteria — applies to any consumer AI product where user perception is the metric that matters.

Case Study  ·  Personal Project

T.ai Teaching Copilot

Automating First-Pass Grading with Human Oversight

Live Demo (available on web & mobile)
TypePersonal project · Prototype
RoleProduct design, system architecture, prototype
UsersProfessors, TAs, university students
ProblemGrading volume scales linearly with enrollment; most TA time goes to rubric scoring, not instruction
OpportunityAI can automate first-pass rubric evaluation with human oversight, freeing TAs for office hours, mentorship, and meaningful instruction
Target MetricsGrading turnaround time · % auto-graded vs. escalated · TA headcount reduction · Feedback consistency · Regrade request rate
01   Problem

Grading scales linearly. Teaching doesn't have to.

As a TA for Data Structures, I saw firsthand: a 500-student course required 20-25 TAs, and the majority of their hours went to rubric scoring — not teaching. Every 25 additional students required roughly one more TA, almost entirely for grading volume. TAs who truly wanted to teach — hold office hours, explain concepts, mentor students — couldn't, because grading consumed most of their hours. Students received feedback weeks later, often just "see rubric."

Most rubric-based grading is pattern matching against defined criteria — exactly where AI performs well. Constrain to the rubric, flag uncertainty, keep humans as final authority.

02   Solution

How the system works.

The copilot automates first-pass rubric scoring so 1-2 TAs can oversee grading for an entire course, spending review time on the 15-25% of submissions that actually need human judgment. Fewer TAs are needed overall, and those who remain can focus on meaningful instruction — office hours, one-on-one support, and helping students actually learn the material.

  • Rubric ingestion: Instructor defines criteria, point allocations, and grading mode (strict for penalizing minor errors, generous for awarding partial credit)
  • AI evaluation: Scores each submission against rubric with individualized, criteria-specific feedback
  • Confidence scoring: Low-confidence submissions auto-route to human review
  • TA review: Approve or override AI grades with one click; edit feedback before it reaches students
  • LMS sync: Approved grades push to Canvas/GradeScope with full audit logs
  • Regrade requests: Students can appeal grades through the LMS; appeals are handled by human TAs only, and regrade patterns feed back into improving the copilot
03   Key Tradeoffs

Tradeoffs that shaped the product.

  • Human-in-the-loop over full automation. A single wrong grade destroys trust. TAs can review and override any AI grade before it reaches students — institutional trust and FERPA compliance over speed.
  • Rubric-constrained over freeform. Constraining AI to instructor-defined criteria makes outputs predictable, auditable, and limits hallucination risk.
  • Confidence-based escalation over random sampling. Focuses human attention on ambiguous submissions and edge cases — fewer reviews, more errors caught.
  • LMS integration over standalone. Fitting into Canvas/GradeScope means zero migration cost, despite higher integration complexity.
04   Prototype

What the interface looks like.

Click any image to enlarge

Dashboard — Live grading status, AI confidence distribution & common mistake detection
Rubric Builder — Criteria definition, point allocation & AI evaluation parameters
Rubric Builder — AI grading modes (Strict / Generous) & escalation threshold configuration
LMS Integration — Canvas & GradeScope sync, grade distribution & feedback preview
LMS Integration — Audit log & data privacy compliance (FERPA, Human Oversight, no training data)
05   What I Learned

Trust is the product.

You don't earn trust by being accurate — you earn it by being transparent about uncertainty. Confidence scores, audit logs, and easy overrides matter more than raw grading accuracy. Framing the value as operational efficiency — fewer TA allocations without sacrificing course quality — resonated more with decision-makers than leading with AI capabilities.

The real impact goes beyond headcount: TAs who used to spend most of their time grading can now spend it teaching.

AI tools in education succeed when they handle volume and let humans handle judgment. The product's job is to draw that line clearly.

Case Study  ·  Start-Up Nation Mentorship

Mentor–Student Matching Engine

Designing a Scalable, Multi-Sector Matching System

TypeProgram infrastructure · Start-Up Nation Mentorship
RoleSystem design, algorithm development, implementation
UsersStudent mentees, professional mentors, program coordinators
ProblemManual mentor–student pairing doesn't scale — match quality degrades as the applicant pool grows
OpportunityWeighted compatibility scoring with human oversight can improve match quality while reducing operational overhead
Key MetricsMatch completion rate · Time-to-match · Post-match engagement · Repeat participation · Satisfaction score
01   Problem

Manual pairing doesn't scale.

Start-Up Nation Mentorship connects 500+ students with professional mentors each year. As the program grew, match quality degraded — misalignment between student goals and mentor expertise, uneven availability across industries, and increasing manual overhead.

If compatibility is formalized into a weighted framework — industry alignment, career goals, skill focus, stated preferences — match satisfaction improves while reducing time-to-match.

02   Approach

A scoring-based system with human oversight.

  • Structured intake forms to capture consistent, scorable signals from both sides of the marketplace
  • Tuned factor weights to reflect which dimensions most reliably predicted match success
  • Built a scoring engine that ranks every possible student–mentor pair, then optimizes assignments across the full cohort — not just greedy top-match-first
  • Surfaced ranked recommendations to coordinators for a single review pass, preserving human judgment as the final layer
03   Key Tradeoffs

A two-sided marketplace with real constraints.

Precision vs Coverage
Optimizing for perfect compatibility reduces match coverage. High-demand industries had far more students than mentors.
Automation vs Human Insight
Over-automation risks reducing the personalization that makes mentorship valuable. The system recommends, not decides.
Speed vs Depth
Rushed matches produce lower engagement. Time-to-match was a metric, not the metric.
Individual Fit vs. Global Fairness
Optimizing for the highest-scoring pairs first can leave remaining participants with weaker options. The system balances top-match quality against equitable outcomes across the full cohort.
04   Outcome

From coordination to infrastructure.

The system supported 500+ annual pairings while reducing coordinator workload from weeks of manual review to a single review pass on algorithmically ranked recommendations. The matching engine was adopted as permanent program infrastructure, running each annual cycle with minimal adjustment.

The hardest part wasn't the algorithm — it was structuring the intake data well enough to make scoring meaningful.

Case Study  ·  Nearwise

Nearwise

The AI companion that knows how your loved one is really doing.

nearwise.xyz
TypeCo-founded startup (team of 3) · Active
RoleHead of Product & Engineering
UsersAging adults (65+), adult children / family caregivers
ProblemFamilies have no window into how aging parents are actually doing day to day
OpportunityA human-sounding AI companion that seniors genuinely enjoy talking to — while surfacing health patterns families would otherwise never see
Key MetricsDaily response rate · Cross-reference alert accuracy · Family engagement rate · Time-to-insight
01   Problem

Families don't lack care. They lack information.

Most adult children talk to aging parents a few times a week. The answer is always "I'm fine." Meanwhile, medications change, meals get skipped, sleep deteriorates, and small symptoms compound — but families have no way to see it happening.

Existing solutions either require too much effort from seniors (apps, wearables) or give families only emergency-level alerts with no daily context. The richest health signals — mood shifts, appetite changes, social withdrawal — go entirely uncollected.

02   Insight

The best health intelligence comes from conversation seniors actually enjoy.

Most seniors already use their phones every day — making calls, sending texts, staying in touch. A conversational AI companion that sounds human, remembers past conversations, and adapts to each person can collect health signals that would otherwise go entirely unnoticed — with zero effort from the senior.

Every conversation matters on its own — but the real power is in what builds up over time. When you connect the dots across days and weeks, you start to see medication side effects, early signs of isolation, cognitive shifts — the kind of patterns that only emerge with consistent, daily contact.

03   Solution

A companion that builds a health picture through conversation.

For seniors: A warm, patient companion who calls and texts throughout the day — remembering past conversations, picking up on their interests, gently checking in on medications and how they're feeling. The health intelligence is extracted behind the scenes; to the senior, it just feels like talking to a friend who always has time.

For families: A clear, actionable daily summary sent straight to their phone — mood, medications, sleep, appetite, and flagged concerns with week-over-week trends. No more guessing. No more "I'm fine." Just real visibility into how their loved one is actually doing.

04   The Intelligence Layer

Behind the scenes, every conversation gets smarter.

The companion drives engagement. The intelligence layer connects dots across days and dimensions — turning natural conversation into patterns families can actually act on.

Medication + Symptoms
Stomach discomfort cross-referenced with a new calcium channel blocker added three days ago — surfaced to the family as a flag worth discussing with their doctor.
Pain → Reduced Activity → Mood Shift
Knee pain Monday. Stopped walking Tuesday. Skipped poker Wednesday. Mood dipped Thursday. No single day is alarming on its own — but the compound picture flags a pattern worth checking on.
05   Key Tradeoffs

Three foundational bets.

  • Conversation as the interface. Unstructured conversation is harder to parse, but it's the only interface seniors will use daily. We accepted backend complexity to keep the senior-facing experience effortless.
  • Phone calls and texts — no app for seniors. Our users are 65+. They don't need to download anything. Nearwise reaches them on the phone they already use — calls and texts only.
  • Companion-first, health tool second. Seniors resist being treated as patients at home. The health monitoring is disguised as a relationship — and the relationship is real. A companion seniors look forward to talking to is the product, not a means to an end.

07   What I Learned

Building for seniors forces better product thinking.

Every interaction has to work on the first try, on the channel they already use, with no onboarding friction. Trust compounds slowly but breaks instantly — one false alert and a family loses confidence in the system. Two-sided products need two distinct value propositions: real visibility and peace of mind for families, genuine companionship and engagement for seniors.
