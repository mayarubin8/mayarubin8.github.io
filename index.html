Case Study  ·  The Humor Project

Computational Humor Research

AI, Language, and Human Judgment

01   Context

Why humor is a hard AI problem.

Humor is one of the hardest human signals to model — it's subjective, contextual, and audience-dependent. This project explored whether AI could generate humor that actually resonates with real users, not just produce clever text.

The work was built into a consumer app used by college students at Columbia, Barnard, and Stanford — which meant the feedback loop was real, fast, and unforgiving.

02   Problem

Optimizing for fluency isn't enough.

Most generative AI systems optimize for correctness or fluency — not emotional response. For humor, that fails.

A joke that works for one audience can fall flat for another. Without clear metrics, it's difficult to know whether an AI system is actually improving or just producing more text.

03   Hypothesis

"If humor is treated as a product problem — not just a language problem — then audience-aware prompting, iterative evaluation, and user feedback can meaningfully improve perceived funniness and engagement."

04   Approach

End-to-end across product and engineering.

Rather than optimizing a single model metric, the system prioritized real user reaction. I worked across the full stack:

  • Evaluated and selected LLMs based on responsiveness, tone control, and iteration speed
  • Designed prompt structures that encode audience context — school, cultural references, conversational tone
  • Defined what a "good joke" means in practice using live user feedback rather than static labels
  • Iterated continuously based on in-app testing and qualitative signals from real student users
05   Evaluation & Metrics

Human judgment over synthetic benchmarks.

Success was measured through signals that actually reflect humor quality — not proxies that are easy to compute but easy to game:

  • User testing and qualitative feedback from real college students
  • Engagement signals within the live consumer app
  • Iterative comparison across prompt strategies
  • Human judgment alignment rather than raw accuracy scores

This intentionally avoided over-optimizing on synthetic benchmarks that don't capture whether something is actually funny.

06   Key Challenges

The difficulty is the point.

Subjectivity
Humor has no objective ground truth. There's no loss function for "funny." Every evaluation decision required judgment.
Ambiguity
User feedback is noisy and inconsistent. Aggregating signal from it without overfitting required careful validation design.
Iteration Cost
Small prompt changes could meaningfully alter outcomes in unpredictable ways — requiring controlled experiments, not intuition.
Audience Specificity
What works at Columbia fails at Stanford. Encoding cultural context into prompts without hardcoding it was an ongoing design challenge.
07   Product Decisions

Tradeoffs made deliberately.

  • Prioritized fast iteration over perfect models — shipping and learning faster than optimizing in isolation
  • Chose live user feedback over static labeled datasets — noisy but more honest signal
  • Accepted ambiguity as a signal rather than a flaw to be resolved away
  • Designed prompts for control and adaptability rather than raw generative creativity
08   Outcome & Learnings

What the project reinforced.

Subjective domains — humor, taste, trust, emotional response — require clear product framing to make tractable. Without defining what "better" means in human terms, iteration is directionless.

Iterative human-in-the-loop evaluation consistently outperformed one-shot optimization. The feedback loop was the product.

AI systems improve most when paired with thoughtful product decisions — not just stronger models. The hardest part wasn't the engineering. It was knowing what to measure.

09   Why This Matters

Humor as a stand-in for harder problems.

Humor is a proxy for many real-world product challenges: trust, taste, emotional response, and subjective value. These are the domains where the difference between a useful product and a technically impressive one is felt most sharply.

Building AI in these domains requires judgment, iteration, and restraint — skills directly transferable to consumer-facing and platform products alike. That is what this project was really about.

AI Teaching Copilot

Re-architecting Academic Evaluation for Scale

01   Origin

Where the idea came from.

While serving as a Teaching Assistant for Data Structures, I noticed that large courses required extensive TA cohorts primarily to manage grading volume.

Most TA time was spent on repetitive rubric-based scoring — not instruction. Scaling feedback meant scaling headcount. The bottleneck wasn't expertise. It was bandwidth.

02   Insight

"AI changes that constraint. If grading workflows are partially automated — with structured human oversight — institutions can standardize rubric application, deliver deeper individualized feedback, reduce redundant grading labor, and reallocate TAs toward teaching and mentorship."

The goal is not replacement. It is leverage.

03   Proposed System

How it works end-to-end.

  • AI performs first-pass grading using structured rubric input
  • Generates individualized, criteria-aligned feedback for each submission
  • Flags ambiguous or edge-case submissions for human review
  • Integrates with LMS platforms — Canvas and GradeScope

1–2 TAs oversee the system, lead recitations, and audit flagged cases. Human judgment remains central — the AI handles volume; humans handle nuance.

04   Prototype

What the interface looks like.

A working prototype built in Figma — three core views: the instructor dashboard, the rubric configuration builder, and the LMS integration panel. Scroll to explore.

Dashboard — Live grading status, AI confidence distribution & common mistake detection
Rubric Builder — Criteria definition, point allocation & AI evaluation parameters
Rubric Builder — AI grading modes (Strict / Generous) & escalation threshold configuration
LMS Integration — Canvas & GradeScope sync, grade distribution & feedback preview
LMS Integration — Audit log & data privacy compliance (FERPA, Human Oversight, no training data)
05   Design Principles

Built around trust and transparency.

Human-in-the-Loop
AI recommends; humans decide. Every grade passes through a review layer before reaching the student. The system augments TA judgment, not replaces it.
Rubric-Constrained Generation
Feedback is generated strictly within the criteria defined by the instructor. No freeform evaluation — structured prompts enforce rubric alignment and reduce scoring variance.
Escalation Thresholds
When the model's confidence is low or a submission falls outside expected patterns, it escalates to human review rather than producing a low-confidence grade.
Bias Mitigation
Structured prompts and blind evaluation (no student identity in the grading context) reduce the risk of systematic scoring bias across demographic groups.
06   Economic Framing

Scale without linear labor growth.

Large TA cohorts exist to handle scale. AI allows scale without linear labor growth.

Even modest reductions in grading overhead across large institutions represent meaningful cost reallocation — while potentially improving consistency and feedback quality simultaneously.

A 500-student course requiring 25 TAs primarily for grading could operate with 2–5 TAs overseeing the AI system — freeing the rest of their time for instruction, office hours, and mentorship where human presence actually matters.

07   What This Demonstrates

AI is most powerful when it augments high-skill roles.

This project reframes instructional infrastructure around leverage, not labor expansion. The opportunity isn't to remove humans from education — it's to redirect their time toward the parts that require human presence.

The same pattern applies broadly: the highest-value applications of AI are not the ones that eliminate skilled roles, but the ones that let skilled people operate at a fundamentally different scale.

Mentor–Student Matching Engine

Designing a Scalable, Multi-Sector Matching System

01   Context

Scaling match quality without scaling overhead.

Start-Up Nation Mentorship connects students with accomplished professional mentors across industries. Each year, 500+ matches are facilitated across sectors including technology, finance, healthcare, and consumer industries.

As the program expanded, the challenge became maintaining match quality without increasing operational complexity. Growth needed a system — not more staff time.

02   Problem

Manual pairing does not scale.

As the applicant pool grew across sectors and experience levels, three structural problems emerged:

  • Misalignment between student goals and mentor expertise — matches felt generic rather than deliberate
  • Uneven mentor availability across industries, creating bottlenecks in high-demand sectors
  • Increasing manual overhead in reviewing, comparing, and pairing hundreds of profiles each cycle

Without structure, growth would compromise both efficiency and match quality simultaneously.

03   Hypothesis

"If compatibility is formalized into a structured, weighted framework — incorporating industry alignment, experience level, skill interests, and goals — match satisfaction and engagement will improve while reducing time-to-match."

04   Approach

A scoring-based system with human oversight.

I designed and implemented a compatibility scoring engine that formalized the matching process end-to-end:

  • Categorized mentors and students by sector, expertise level, and professional interests
  • Defined and weighted compatibility factors — industry alignment, career goals, skill focus, stated preferences
  • Standardized intake data structures to improve signal quality and reduce noise in scoring
  • Automated initial pairing recommendations while preserving a human review layer for final decisions

The system was designed to augment judgment, not replace it.

05   Marketplace Constraints

A two-sided problem with real tradeoffs.

This wasn't a simple search problem — it was a two-sided marketplace with supply imbalances, preference asymmetries, and competing objectives:

Precision vs Coverage
Optimizing for perfect compatibility reduces overall match coverage. Certain high-demand industries had far more students than mentors, requiring deliberate supply management.
Automation vs Human Insight
Over-automation risked reducing the personalization that makes mentorship valuable. The system needed to recommend, not decide.
Speed vs Match Depth
Faster matching means less time per pair — but rushed matches produce lower engagement and shorter relationships. Time-to-match was a metric, not the metric.
Cold-Start Problem
New participants with limited profile data produced weak signals. The system needed graceful degradation for sparse inputs rather than failing silently.
06   Metrics

Measuring durable relationships, not just volume.

The objective was high-value mentorship relationships — not match count. Success was evaluated through:

  • Successful match completion rate — did the pairing result in an active relationship?
  • Time-to-match — operational efficiency across the full matching cycle
  • Post-match engagement — meeting frequency, session completion, depth of interaction
  • Repeat participation — mentors and students returning for subsequent cycles
  • Qualitative satisfaction feedback — from both sides of the match
07   Key Challenges

Where the system had to be careful.

  • Cross-sector complexity — mentors often held experience across multiple industries, making clean categorization difficult
  • Cold-start participants — new users with limited profile data required fallback scoring logic
  • Avoiding bias toward high-demand sectors — without correction, popular industries would dominate and underserved sectors would stagnate
  • Transparency in match logic — coordinators needed to understand and trust why a specific pairing was recommended
08   Outcome

From coordination to infrastructure.

The matching infrastructure supported 500+ annual mentor–student pairings while reducing operational friction and improving structured compatibility across the program.

Matching evolved from a manual coordination task into a repeatable, scalable system that grew with the program rather than constraining it.

09   What I Learned

Compatibility is probabilistic, not binary.

Two-sided matching systems require explicit prioritization logic. Without it, the system optimizes for the wrong thing — usually volume over value, or one side of the market over the other.

Tradeoff decisions need to be made consciously and documented. A system that silently compromises on match quality to hit coverage targets is worse than one that surfaces the tradeoff clearly.

Structured input data is often the hardest part. The algorithm is secondary to the quality of signals it receives. Garbage in, garbage out — and in a two-sided marketplace, that cost compounds on both sides simultaneously.
